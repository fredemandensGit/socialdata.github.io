Why should people bike?

We all know that exercising is good for your body, and this also matter when it comes to biking. As noted by Statista, biking can help protect you from serious diseases such as stroke, heart attack, some cancers, depression, diabetes, obesity and arthritis- But even with the spectacular health benefits of biking, we only see that one percent of all trips that Americans take—to work, to the store, on vacation—are by bike, compared with 87 percent by car or truck. Furthermore, by biking, we also help battle the climate change, one trip at a time. Transportation is the largest source of green house emissions, and cars and light trucks account for 58 percent of transportation emissions. Our goal is therefore to help both the earth but also the individual by making it more attractive to bike! In this project we will examine the patterns of bike sharing in Washington DC by investigating a dataset from the bike-sharing company Capital Bikeshare along with a dataset containing weather data. The data is recorded in a 2 year period from 2011 to 2012. We have chosen to work with these 2 datasets to enable an investigation of temporal patterns in weather data and number of rented bikes along with an analysis of geo data of the bike stations in the city. Finally, we will create two models to predict the bike count on unseen data.

By investigating and learning about bike patterns we can, from the perspective of a bikesharing company, aim to make bike sharing more convenient for the customers. In particular, we must ensure there is always a sufficient supply of bikes to be rented and enough bike-stations around the city. So let’s investigate how we can help both the people and the earth, one rental bike at a time.

Data introduction

As mentioned in the introduction we work with data from Capital Bikeshare, a bike-rental company in Washington D.C.

This dataset consists of recordings of every bike rental made from 2011 to 2012. The data holds information about the duration of the trip, start and stop stations and member type. In total, the dataset consists of more than 3 million bike trips. Furthermore, we do also include another dataset with hourly recordings of the weather also from 2011 to 2012. The weather data consists of various weather-attributes such as temperature, feeling temperature, humidity, windspeeds and different weather situations.

Data exploration

Let’s investigate the data. First, we will investigate the weather data. Below is an interactive figure with two tabs: Temperature and Weather Situations. Each tab contains a histogram which displays the bike count for each category. Furthermore, the fraction of the bike count for each category can be explored by mousing over each bin. Let’s dive in!

Bokeh Plot

Looking at the Temperature histogram, we can observe that largest fraction of bikes is rented when the temperature is between 20 and 30 degrees Celsius (approx. 50%) while the lowest fraction of rented bikes is when the temperature is between 40-50 or between -20 and -10 degrees Celsius. This seems reasonable as it makes sense that more people bike when the weather is not too hot or too cold. Furthermore, this means the bike sharing company must be prepared to have a large supply of bikes available when the temperature is between 10 and 30 degrees Celsius as this is when a lot of people tends to rent bikes.

Looking at the Weather Situations histogram, it can be observed that 70% of all bike rentals happen when the weather is clear whereas 25% is when the weather is misty/cloudy. This tells us that most people rent bikes when the weather is dry and not rainy. Therefore, maintenance and repair of the bikes should preferably happen when it rains since most bikes are being rented in such weather. Now we will switch gears and investigate the temporal patterns of bike rental. Again, an interactive figure is provided with the following tabs: Weekday, 24hour cycle and Seasonal.

Bokeh Plot

The weekday histogram shows that there is very little difference in the bike count across the weekdays. Initially, we thought that more bikes would be rented in the weekends, but this does not seem to be the case. That made us interested in looking into the fraction between membership users and single trip users. We discovered that 81% of all the rented bikes is by people who are members while only 19% is by people taking a single trip. Therefore, we now assume that the rented bikes are mainly used for everyday commute to and from work which fits the almost uniform distribution displayed in the weekday histogram.

In the 24hour cycle histogram we observe a bimodal distribution. The first peak is in the morning hours (07:00 - 09:00) while the second peak is in the afternoon hours (16:00-19:00). In the night time we observe very few rented bikes. This correlates well with our assumption made from the weekday histogram that the rented bikes are mainly used for daily commute. From a company perspective that means the supply of available bikes should be large in the daytime to ensure as many people as possible rent a bike.

In the seasonal histogram, we can observe that the season with most bike rentals is summer followed by spring, fall and then winter. This corresponds well with what we observed in the temperature- and weather situations histograms where we saw that people do not like to bike in bad weather. However, the fractions of bike rentals in summer, spring and fall are 0.32, 0.27 and 0.25 which is quite even.

Heatmaps

Now will take a step back and try to get an overview of where in Washington D.C. bikes are being rented. To give us a good overview of just that we will use heat maps. Below is a heat map of Washington D.C where we have plotted the location of all bike rentals from 2011 to 2012. The coloring indicates the number of bikes being rented. Red areas represent a lot of bike rentals while light blue areas represent a low number of bike rentals.

The above heat map shows the use of the bike sharing stations from 2011-2012. It can be observed that the area with most rented bikes is the neighborhood of Foggy Bottom which is marked with a green circle. That neighborhood includes the campus of George Washington University, and it is located close to downtown Washington DC and historic sights such as Lincoln Memorial. That can explain why Foggy Bottom is the area with largest use of bike rental. The general trend in the map is that the largest number of rented bikes is in the center of the city whereas the bike count fades out when you move out of the city. From a company perspective, that means most efforts should be focused in and around the downtown area. It could also be interesting to look at the location of bike rentals across the 24hours cycle. To do that we have created a heat map movie which can be seen below:

Playing the heat map above we can observe how the number of rented bikes shifts between neighborhoods. Generally, the movie corresponds well to the 24hour histogram displayed in section 3.1. We can observe low activity in the night hours except for a few hotspots, a peak of activity around 08:00 to 10:00, a little dip of activity around 10:00 to 15:00, another peak at 16:00 to 19:00 and finally low activity in the late evening hours. This trend corresponds well to the bimodal distribution. Looking at the Foggy Bottom neighborhood (green circle) we can see the activity in the neighborhood grows steadily from late morning, peaks in the afternoon rush hour and then cools off in the evening. Two other neighborhoods worth mentioning are the Dupont Cirlce (red circle) and Shaw (blue circle) neighborhood which is located northeast of Foggy Bottom. Those two neighborhoods show high activity at odd times such as during the night. Especially Dupont Cirlce shows high activity from 09:00 to the end of the day. However, according to [4] dupont circle is a focal point connecting highly trafficated roads which can explain why the activity is high also on odd times. Whereas the majority of the population in Shaw neighborhood is aged 22-39 which can also explain high activity in bike rental at nighttime. Now that we have a better insight of the bike patterns in Washington DC, we will investigate how we can utilize machine learning models to predict the bike rental count.

Prediction

We will introduce a Random Forest regressor for bike prediction. A rough sketch of the process: We took all the recordings from the bike dataset and randomly split that data into 2 chunks- a test and training set. The size ratio of the training and test set is 70/30 respectively. The models were then trained on the training set together with the weather data. Afterwards the test set were fed into the models to perform predictions. The technical details on creating, training, and testing the models can be found in the explainer notebook. Let’s look at the predictions

The above graph shows only a subset of the test set and predictions since including all observations looks messy. We can observe that the random forest resembles the pattern of the test data quite well. The main weakness of the random forest model is that it fails to capture the high peaks in the test data. However, we computed the coefficient of determination, R2, to be ~0.92 which is pretty good. Moreover, we can investigate which features the random forest regressor deems most important in the following graph:

We can see that the most important feature for the random forest regressor is the hour-feature which tells us the hour of the day whereas the next most important features are temperature, feeling temperature (ie. atemp) and humidity. The fact that these features are the most important to predict the bike rental count supports the analysis we made earlier where we saw significant patterns in the 24hour cycle and temperature histogram. Therefore, from a company perspective it is crucially to look out for weather forecasts and plan for each hour to ensure supply and maintenance.

Conclusion

To summarize, we have investigated the bike rental patterns in Washington D.C. where we have looked at temporal patterns and weather features. We have found that most bike rentals happen when the temperature is between 10 and 30 degrees Celsius with no rain. Moreover, have we identified that most bikes are being rented in morning and afternoon hours during weekdays while most rentals appear during noon in weekends. We have also found that the neighborhood of Foggy Bottom is where most bikes are being rented and that neighborhoods of Shaw and Dupont Circle have a lot of rentals during evening and nighttime. Finally, we succeed in creating an accurate Random Forest regressor to predict the number of bikes being rented at each hour. All of these findings can be utilized by a bikesharing company like Capital Bikeshare to ensure there’s always enough bikes at all bike stations around the city so that more people can bike. This way peoples health can be improved along with the creation of a better climate.
